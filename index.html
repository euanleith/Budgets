All of the opposition parties are proposing increases in housing expenditure.

The parties can be roughly split into three groups;

  • The incumbent parties Fianna Fáil, Fine Gael, and Green Party (FFG) spending around €4B.
  • Sinn Féin (SF), Labour, and Social Democrats (SD) proposing increases in expenditure of around €1-1.6B.
  • People Before Profit (PBP) proposing a significantly greater increase in expenditure of around €4.7B.

The vast majority of expenditure from all parties is towards existing schemes. However, in the next few sections we will focus on the changes that have been proposed as they provide some interesting insights.

HAP/RAS

Summary

All parties are proposing to significantly reduce expenditure in the long run, as new social housing fulfills those needs. They have all acknowledged the benefits of the scheme. However, the opposition parties note that despite being considered a short term fix, spending has increased significantly over time. The opposition parties claim that the government isn't building enough new social housing to replace these schemes; while the incumbent parties claim that significantly more houses are being built each year, and that the crisis is already starting to be fixed.

Detail

All parties are proposing to significantly reduce expenditure in the long run as new social housing fulfills those housing needs

All parties have acknowledged the benefits of the schemes in directly addressing high rent prices[citation needed]. In fact, the opposition parties believe that these policies are currently underfunded, or should be reviewed for underfunding in the short term, as rent prices have increased, meaning fewer tenants can make use of these schemes.

However, HAP/RAS are considered only short term measures, to be replaced in the long run with new social housing. Since these schemes don't result in any new housing, and a lack of housing is believed to be the most important factor in the increase in house prices, they don't actually solve the underlying problem[FFG citation needed]. Furthermore, they are believed to be poor value for money in the long run compared to funding new social housing due to their large ongoing costs[FFG citation needed].

The opposition parties take issue with the implementation of these schemes;

  • They believe these schemes have been depended on for too long[citation needed]. Despite being ostensibly short term measures, spending towards these schemes has increased significantly over time; money which could have been spent on new social housing and other such schemes which reduce rent prices. This attitude has also been expressed by the Housing Commission in their 2024 annual report[citation needed].
  • Sinn Féin have claimed that they hide the severity of the crisis. As HAP/RAS tenancies fall under the legal definition of council housing, they are excluded from figures for social housing needs. However, short term rent supplement recipients are still included in these figures, despite both programmes not providing for long term housing needs. Therefore this distinction is arbitrary, and is only being used to hide to severity of the crisis. todo this also removes them from the waiting list for social housing, which means they'll be stuck with HAP, and if they get kicked out they have to go through a bunch of paperwork to get on social housing.

However the incumbent parties reject these claims;

  • They claim that continued spending towards these schemes has been necessary due to high rent prices. Removing them 'would have a devasting impact on the thousands of people currently availing of such schemes.'
  • They have faced challenges building new social housing due to Covid-19. This resulted in restrictions in construction sites, which impacted the number of houses built in 2020 and 2021.
  • However they believe that the crisis is already starting to be fixed. They are building significantly more new social housing in recent years, which will presumably lead to HAP/RAS spending being reduced in the long run.

Note that there has been some disagreement within the incumbent parties about the implementation of these schemes. A TD for the Green Party has stated 'there's opposition to what we're doing, even within Government' and a TD for Fianna Fáil has called HAP/RAS spending levels 'incredibly poor value for money'. As the incumbent parties are in coalition, their combined budget expresses a unified position on the issue. However, the release of each parties' pre-election manifesto in the near future will hopefully clarify any differences.
