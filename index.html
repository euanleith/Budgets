Housing Budgets

A series of graphs comparing the 2024 housing budgets of Irish political parties

Description

This chart shows the total housing expenditure for each party.

Overview

All of the opposition parties are proposing increases in housing expenditure.

The parties can be roughly split into three groups; the incumbent parties Fianna Fáil, Fine Gael, and the Green Party spending around €4B; Sinn Féin, Labour, and the Social Democrats proposing increases in expenditure of around €1.5B; and People Before Profit proposing a significantly greater increase in expenditure of around €4.7B.

Description

This chart shows a more detailed view of individual policies, which have been sorted into groups for clarity. Information regarding each policy can be seen be hovering over them with the mouse. Groups can be hidden by clicking their name in the legend, and isolated by double-clicking their name.

Overview

The most significant policy group is new social housing, with both the highest expenditure by all parties, and the greatest difference in expenditure between parties.

Discussion

New Social Housing

Definition

Policies which fund the building or acquisition of properties for use as social housing.

Summary of discussion

All parties support an increase in social housing to support low-income households and reduce homelessness. However, the opposition parties have also expressed their intention to increase supply as a means to reducing house prices. The opposition parties believe that the current targets for new social housing are too low. Furthermore, they note that targets from previous years haven't been met. The incumbent parties have stated their intention to increase their targets in line with recent reports, and have cited a number of roadblocks which have impeded development. They have also noted that despite this, there has still been a significant increase in delivery of social housing under their government.

All parties have expressed the benefits of social housing;

  • Meets the needs of low-income families and is a key enabler of people moving out of homelessness[opposition citations needed?]. Without sufficient social housing, those who can't afford housing will be forced into homelessness, and low-income households won't have enough income to fulfill their other needs after paying their rent or mortgage. This benefit is also expressed by the opposition parties.
  • The opposition parties have also expressed their intention to increase social housing as a means to reducing house prices[SD citation needed]. This follows the logic that increasing supply relative to demand will cause prices to fall. The incumbent parties haven't explicitly cited social housing as a means to reducing house prices. Furthermore they have expressed concerns regarding the reduction of house prices at a fast rate; preferring a case where 'incomes rise faster than house prices'.

However, the opposition parties believe that the targets set by the incumbent parties are too low, each party having proposed higher targets in their budgets. And more recently, various parties have also cited reports from independent bodies which have proposed higher targets, such as The Housing Commission, The Department of Finance (DoF), and the Economic and Social Research Institute (ESRI).

  • The incumbent parties have since stated that they plan to update targets in accordance with a more recent ESRI report; although their proposed targets are lower than the those determined by the Housing Commission and the DoF.
  • Despite this, Sinn Féin and the Social Democrats still take issue with these new targets. The target for new housing is determined in part by the predicted housing demand over time. These parties claim that this calculation in the report doesn't take into account the current shortage of housing for the existing population, and only considers future increases in demand from an increase in population.

Furthermore, the opposition parties note that current targets aren't being met[opposition citations needed?]. Every year since 2020 the targets for new social housing have been missed. And the opposition parties have expressed concerns about meeting this year's targets, with only 158 of the targeted 9,300 units built.

  • The incumbent parties have cited a number of roadblocks which have impeded development, such as labour shortages and supply issues due to Covid-19. Such problems present more complexity than simply increasing capital funding, and can take time to fix. For more info see Section 3 of the incumbent parties' housing plan.
  • Despite this, they claim that they are still delivering a significant amount of social housing. They claim that 'the strongest national delivery of social housing since 1975 was recorded in 2022 [...], and this record was exceeded again in 2023'. In response, Sinn Féin have claimed that since the 70's 'the population has doubled, and the level of need has increased'.
  • They also claim that they will reach their targets this year. As of July 2024 there are '26,000 social homes at various different stages of construction'. They have also assuaged criticism of the low figures in the first quarter of the year, claiming that construction is 'heavily weighted towards the last quarter'.

Description

This chart shows the savings made on policies which parties have proposed to remove, and proposed expenditure towards policies which parties plan to remove or cut back in the future.

Overview

All parties have proposed some cut backs or removal of policies, immediately or in the future; with RAS & HAP constituting the highest cost.

The opposition parties believe that the money currently spent on these policies would be better used on new social housing, generally due to believed inefficiencies or claims that they don't reduce market house prices.

Discussion

Rental Accommodation Scheme (RAS) and Housing Assistance Payments (HAP)

Definition

Long term rent supplement paid by local authorities to the landlord. See here for more info on RAS and HAP.

Summary of discussion

Spending towards these schemes has increased significantly over time, despite being considered short term measures, as they don't reduce house prices. The opposition parties claim that the government isn't building enough new social housing to replace these schemes. The incumbent parties disagree, claiming that there has been a significant increase in the delivery of new social housing, which will presumably relieve dependency on these schemes.

All parties have acknowledged the benefits of the schemes in directly addressing high rent prices. In fact, the opposition parties believe that these policies are currently underfunded, or should be reviewed for underfunding in the short term, as rent prices have increased, meaning fewer tenants can make use of these schemes.

However, HAP/RAS are considered only short term measures, to be replaced in the long run with new social housing. Since these schemes don't result in any new housing, and a lack of housing is believed to be the most important factor in the increase in house prices, they don't actually solve the underlying problem[FFG citation needed]. Furthermore, they are believed to be poor value for money in the long run compared to funding new social housing due to their large ongoing costs[FFG citation needed].

The opposition parties take issue with the implementation of these schemes;

  • They believe these schemes have been depended on for too long. Despite being ostensibly short term measures, spending towards these schemes has increased significantly over time; money which could have been spent on new social housing and other such schemes which reduce rent prices. This attitude has also been expressed by the Housing Commission in their 2024 annual report.
  • Sinn Féin have claimed that they hide the severity of the crisis. As HAP/RAS tenancies fall under the legal definition of council housing, they are excluded from figures for social housing needs. However, short term rent supplement recipients are still included in these figures, despite both programmes not providing for long term housing needs. Therefore this distinction is arbitrary, and is only being used to hide to severity of the crisis.

However the incumbent parties reject these claims;

  • They claim that continued spending towards these schemes has been necessary due to high rent prices. Removing them 'would have a devasting impact on the thousands of people currently availing of such schemes.'
  • They have faced challenges building new social housing due to Covid-19. This resulted in restrictions in construction sites, which impacted the number of houses built in 2020 and 2021.
  • However they believe that the crisis is already starting to be fixed. They are building significantly more new social housing in recent years, which will presumably lead to HAP/RAS spending being reduced in the long run.

Note that there has been some disagreement within the incumbent parties about the implementation of these schemes. A TD for the Green Party has stated 'there's opposition to what we're doing, even within Government' and a TD for Fianna Fáil has called HAP/RAS spending levels 'incredibly poor value for money'. As the incumbent parties are in coalition, their combined budget expresses a unified position on the issue. However, the release of each parties' pre-election manifesto in the near future will hopefully clarify any differences.

Croí Cónaithe (Cities)

Definition

A fund to support the building of apartments for sale to owner-occupiers, where the cost of development is greater than the market sale price. See here for more info on Croí Cónaithe (Cities).

Summary of discussion

The incumbent parties claim the scheme promotes development; while Sinn Féin, Social Democrats, and Labour take issue with the fact that affordable prices are not a condition of funding, meaning it doesn't fix the underlying price issue of the housing crisis. Labour proposes to add this condition, while Sinn Féin and the Social Democrats believe that the money would be better spent on new social and affordable housing.

The incumbent parties claim that this scheme increases development in cities by bridging the gap between the cost of development and the sale price. Without bridging this gap, fewer developers will be willing to build new houses. And more houses in cities will 'help to tackle climate change, reduce vacancy and dereliction and help to meet the strong demand for urban living'.

Opposition arguments;

  • Sinn Féin, Social Democrats, and Labour take issue with the fact that affordable prices are not a condition of funding. Without this, private developers can receive substantial subsidies while still charging unaffordable prices. Labour proposes to add this condition, while Sinn Féin and the Social Democrats believe that the money would be better spent on new social and affordable housing.
  • Sinn Féin claims that the cost per unit has been greater than estimated. They claim that in 2022 cost per unit was estimated to be around €80,000; this has increased to €135,000 in 2024. Therefore either funding will have to increase to meet the housing target, or the target will have to be decreased.
  • Sinn Féin claims that the current progress doesn't seem to be on track to reach targets. As of July 2024, contracts have been signed for the building of 582 units, while the target is to deliver 5,000 units by 2026. Sinn Féin claims that 'given that apartment developments typically take two years to complete, time is running out for contracts to be signed to meet the target'.

First Home Shared Equity & Help to Buy Schemes

Definitions

These schemes help residents who are buying or building their first home. Under Help to Buy this is in the form of a tax refund. And under the First Home Scheme, the government and participating banks pay up to 30% of the cost of the home in return for a stake in it. This stake can be bought back by the resident at any time. See here for more info on the First Home Scheme and Help to Buy.

Summary of discussion

The incumbent parties claim that these schemes save buyers money, and help tenants get out of expensive rental accommodation. Sinn Féin, the Social Democrats, and Labour claim they don't reduce the cost of housing, or even cause prices to increase, and therefore don't fix the underlying problem of the housing crisis. They believe therefore that the money would be better spent on new social and affordable housing.

The incumbent parties claim that if not for the schemes, many people would remain in 'unsecure rental accommodation, often times at more cost than a mortgage would be'., stating 'you have to support people to get their [mortgage] deposit together'.

Opposition arguments;

  • Sinn Féin, the Social Democrats, and Labour claim that the schemes don't address problem of house prices. Since the schemes don't increase supply of housing, and a lack of housing is believed to be the most important factor in the increase in house prices, they don't actually solve the underlying problem. These parties therefore believe that the money would be better spent on new social and affordable housing.
  • In fact, Sinn Féin, the Social Democrats, and Labour claim that the First Home scheme pushes up house prices. They cite reports from the Central Bank of Ireland and London School of Economics, which have shown that a similar scheme in England has increased house prices. These reports conclude that the scheme has mostly helped middle-income households buy more expensive houses than they would otherwise, rather than helping low-income renting households to get a mortgage, since house prices are still too high even with government assistance. This means that buyers spend more while demand for new houses doesn't increase, which causes 'upward price pressures' for the wider market. Sinn Féin also claims that according to experts, the same is the case for the Help to Buy scheme.
  • Sinn Féin claim that the Help to Buy scheme is available to buyers who don't need the support. They claim that according to an Oireachtas Budgetary Services report, a third of recipients did not need the scheme to meet their 10% mortgage deposit requirement, and could instead use the scheme to create larger deposits.
  • Sinn Féin has expressed concerns with the level of interest on loans under the First Home scheme. They claim that 'over a 30-year period the cost of the debt will amount to €23,800 on a €50,000 equity loan'.

Social Housing Public-Private Partnership Programme

Definition

A public-private partnership (PPP) is an arrangement between a public authority and a private partner designed to deliver a public infrastructure project or service under a long-term contract. In the case of this PPP project, the private partner will finance and construct the homes, then provide maintenance and tenancy management services for a 25 year service period with the homes maintained to a pre-defined standard. The homes remain in local authority ownership.

Summary of discussion
