Comparing the 2024 housing budgets of Irish political parties

All of the opposition parties are proposing increases in housing expenditure.

The parties can be roughly split into three groups; FFG spending around €4B, Sinn Fein, Labour, and Social Democrats proposing increases in expenditure of around €1-1.6B, and People Before Profit proposing a significantly greater increase in expenditure of around €4.7B.

The most significant spending group is new social housing, with both the highest expenditure and difference in expenditure between parties.

Again, FFG is spending the least at €1.8B, followed by SF, Labour and SocDems proposing increases of around €1-1.5B, and PBP proposing a greater increase of around €3.6B.

Policies which are proposed to receive reduced spending or removal/reduction in the future can be seen on the positive side of the chart, as they are still being paid for in this year's budget.

Policies which are proposed to be removed can be seen in the negative side of the chart, as they are no longer contributing to the total expenditure. However it should be noted that a lack of expenditure is not the same as new income.

Some of the arguments for and against these policies will be explored below:

HAP/RAS

The opposition parties are all proposing to remove or significantly reduce expenditure towards the HAP and RAS schemes in the long run, to be replaced with increased funding towards new social housing (todo source). The incumbent parties have maintained their confidence in the schemes.

The stated arguments against the schemes are as follows:

  • They don't increase the total stock of housing. The lack of housing is a significant factor in the increase in house prices, so HAP/RAS don't actually solve the problem of increased house prices.
  • They leave the government with no long term asset (socdems budget end of pg22, labour budget pg37, also socdems email). Money that is spent on these policies is lost, rather than being maintained in the form of an asset, as would be the case is this money was instead used to fund new government owned social housing.
  • The value for money is claimed to be worse than funding new social housing. The repeated long term cost of HAP/RAS is believed to outweigh the single short term cost of building/acquiring new social housing. (socdems budget end of pg22, sf - https://vote.sinnfein.ie/gap-in-hap-thresholds-and-rent-rates-highlights-how-government-reliance-on-private-rented-sector-is-failing-tenants-eoin-o-broin-td/, also socdems email)
  • Tenants under HAP/RAS don't have long-term security of tenure (https://www.sinnfein.ie/housing-4). This reduces tenants' peace of mind regarding their finances and security, as well as the feeling of having a 'home'.
  • todo remove? They potentially cause rents to increase. Through HAP/RAS the government is effectively competing on behalf of renters under these schemes against private renters for the limited stock of existing leases. This increased competition without a proportional increase in supply is proposed to cause prices to rise. (see socdems email, https://businessplus.ie/news/hap-rents/ (not a good source)). And further, this creates a vicious cycle where rents are increased, which requires HAP/RAS spending to increase, which increases rents further.
  • They're hard to be stopped once they're started. If these schemes were to stop tomorrow, recipients would no longer be able to afford rent, and many would become homeless. Therefore, the government is required to maintain a high level of spending to avoid this outcome. Because of this, the opposition parties are proposing spending reductions over the long run (see socdems email, other...) todo also people who are currently on HAP/RAS will be personally incentivised to vote for parties who maintain the programme, because removing the programmes will put their renting situation into question.
